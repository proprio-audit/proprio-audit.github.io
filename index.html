Abstract

Vision-based attacks on Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have received increasing attention as these policies are rapidly adopted for robotic control. However, the vulnerability of non-visual inputs, particularly proprioception, remains underexplored. We present ProprioAudit, a framework for studying inference-time adversarial attacks and defenses targeting the proprioceptive input channel of VLAs. Our attack leverages the velocity field of the VLA action head to optimize small proprioceptive perturbations that induce significant task failures across representative VLAs in both simulation and real-world settings. We introduce a defense based on stochastic proprioceptive conditioning, which gives the model an internal mechanism for comparing proprioception-conditioned and proprioception-independent action predictions. We show that this structure enables accurate and rapid detection of adversarial proprioceptive inputs. Our results show that proprioceptive conditioning introduces a largely undefended attack surface, and the multimodal structure of VLAs can be leveraged as an effective defense mechanism.
